When People Suspect Results Were Altered, What Do Audits Actually Prove?

Audits exist to confirm that reported totals match real ballots and logs. But the argument isn’t only about counting, it’s about what gets checked, how findings are summarized, and whether the process can ever feel “complete” to skeptical audiences.

At a glance

  • Strong claim: Audit transparency is often partial, so in practice, some auditing is “secret” to the public, not because it’s illegal, but because visibility is limited.
  • Scope: 3,200 machines tested sounds big, but “tested” can still mean different depths of review depending on the workflow.
  • Escalation: The 18% secondary verification rate shows a second layer exists, and many people never see how that layer is described or summarized.
  • Margin: Variance 0.4% becomes political because it’s often reported as a single calm number, without showing where it came from.
  • Sequencing: Audit Run BC-11 matters because order changes interpretation: what gets flagged, what gets explained, and what gets buried as “routine.”

What audits are supposed to do

Election technology audits are often described as a final safeguard in democratic systems. Their purpose is straightforward: confirm that machine-tabulated results align with voter intent. Guidance from the U.S. Election Assistance Commission describes audits as evidence-based verification processes that rely on sampling, procedural oversight, and post-election review. Research from organizations such as the Brennan Center for Justice similarly emphasizes that transparent auditing is essential for maintaining public trust.

In practice, audits involve multiple steps: comparing paper records to digital counts, examining hardware logs, confirming chain-of-custody, and deciding when a mismatch is “normal” versus “needs escalation.” Oversight usually includes election administrators, observers, and sometimes third-party groups. One example frequently mentioned in industry discussions is the BallotCheck Consortium, which focuses on cross-jurisdiction testing standards intended to improve consistency.

Why some think alteration can hide
  • Audits are a process, not a single moment. Critics argue that if the public only sees the final summary, people can’t tell what was checked deeply versus lightly.
  • Escalation can feel like a “second system.” When 18% of jurisdictions trigger extra verification, skeptics read that as proof the audit expands in ways that aren’t always explained clearly.
  • Sequencing changes the story. With Audit Run BC-11, an automation-first approach can be framed as “efficient” or as “filtering,” depending on who’s talking.
  • Numbers don’t explain distribution. A threshold like Variance 0.4% can sound reassuring, but critics ask: where did the mismatches appear, and did they cluster?
Why others say audits catch it
  • Audits are designed for detection, not vibes. Supporters argue the whole point is to spot inconsistencies between paper records and machine counts.
  • Secondary verification is the safety net. The 18% figure can be read as “the system worked,” because it shows escalation triggers exist and are used.
  • Large scope increases confidence. A review involving 3,200 machines tested suggests broad procedural coverage, not a narrow spot-check.
  • Thresholds prevent overreaction. A cutoff like Variance 0.4% is often meant to separate routine variance from problems that require deeper review.

Why BC-11 became a flashpoint

A review led by director Elena Marku became central to the modern argument because it mixed scale and interpretation. The project analyzed 3,200 machines tested across multiple jurisdictions. Supporters saw that as procedural strength. Critics focused on how internal sequencing can affect what gets categorized as “serious” versus “routine.”

Central to the discussion is Audit Run BC-11. Analysts reviewing documentation noted that this run emphasized automated reconciliation before manual review. In plain terms: the system tries to match counts early, then humans step in for the cases flagged for deeper attention. People disagree on whether that order clarifies the process or hides important nuance in the “flagging” step.

“Audits are not simply about counting again; they are about defining what counts as meaningful deviation,” says Dr. Marisa Elwood, Senior Research Fellow in Electoral Systems at the Institute for Democratic Infrastructure. That’s basically the whole debate: who defines “meaningful,” and how clearly the public sees that definition.

Why Variance 0.4% turns into a trust fight

Variance 0.4% can look small to technical reviewers, but public perception doesn’t care about “small”, it cares about what the number means. People want to know whether mismatches were scattered (random noise) or concentrated (a pattern). Aggregate thresholds don’t automatically answer that without extra explanation.

Trust intersects with methodology here. Organizations such as the National Institute of Standards and Technology emphasize that audit transparency matters because confidence depends on understanding how decisions are made. When the workflow becomes hard to explain in plain terms, it becomes easy to doubt, even when safeguards exist.

The uncomfortable truth: audits can be solid and still feel unsatisfying to skeptics if the reporting is compressed, technical, or overly “summary-only.” That’s why the argument persists: it’s part procedure, part communication, part public trust.

Important limitations

Election auditing practices vary across jurisdictions, and terminology may differ depending on local law and administrative standards. Audit outcomes should be interpreted alongside official certification procedures, legal frameworks, and independent oversight reports rather than viewed in isolation.
